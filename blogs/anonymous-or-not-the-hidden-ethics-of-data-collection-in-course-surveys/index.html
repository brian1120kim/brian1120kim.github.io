“Anonymous… or Not?”: The Hidden Ethics of Data Collection in Course Surveys

Brian Kim | Jun 29, 2026 min read

Anonymous… or Not?

In today’s data-driven world, organizations rely heavily on feedback mechanisms such as surveys to evaluate performance and guide improvements. On the surface, course evaluation surveys appear harmless—simple tools used to gather participant insights to enhance learning experiences. However, when we examine these systems through a data science ethics lens, deeper concerns emerge around privacy, transparency, fairness, and accountability.

This blog post explores a course evaluation survey that claims anonymity despite requiring user login credentials. By analyzing its design and intended use, we uncover ethical dilemmas and propose actionable recommendations grounded in core ethical principles such as informed consent, anonymity vs. confidentiality, bias, data governance, and professional responsibility.

Understanding the Scenario

At the end of a course, participants are asked to complete a 10-question survey. The survey is accessed through a web link and requires users to log in with their organizational username and password. Despite this, the survey explicitly states that responses are anonymous and will remain so.

Executive management intends to use the survey results to evaluate courses and determine which ones should be redesigned or updated.

At first glance, this approach appears reasonable and efficient. However, a closer look reveals several ethical concerns that could significantly affect both the quality of the data collected and the trust of participants.

1. Ethical Issues in the Survey Design

The most immediate ethical issue lies in the misleading claim of anonymity. While the survey promises that responses are anonymous, requiring login credentials makes true anonymity impossible. Anonymity means that no responses can be linked back to individuals, whereas confidentiality allows identities to exist but keeps them protected. Because login information introduces the potential for responses to be traced—even indirectly—the survey is not truly anonymous; it is merely confidential.

This misrepresentation directly violates the principle of informed consent. Participants may feel comfortable sharing honest feedback under the belief that their identities are completely protected. However, if this assumption is incorrect, they are making decisions based on incomplete or misleading information about how their data is handled.

Another key issue is the power imbalance within the organization. Participants may feel obligated to complete the survey or may hesitate to provide negative feedback due to fear of potential consequences, even if those fears are not explicitly justified. This environment fosters social desirability bias, where individuals tailor their responses to appear more favorable rather than being fully honest. As a result, the data collected may not accurately reflect participants’ true opinions, which undermines the integrity of the analysis.

Additionally, poor survey design can further compromise the reliability of the results. Issues such as leading questions or vague wording can influence responses or create ambiguity. If participants interpret questions differently or feel guided toward certain answers, the resulting data loses its accuracy. This directly impacts data integrity, as the survey no longer captures a truthful representation of participant experiences.

2. Additional Ethical Issues in the Survey

Beyond the survey’s design, several broader ethical concerns must be considered. One major issue is data privacy. Because participants must log in, there is a possibility that responses could be linked to individuals through metadata such as timestamps, IP addresses, or system logs. Without strong safeguards, this data could be accessed or misused in unintended ways. This underscores the importance of data governance, which ensures that clear policies are in place to regulate how data is collected, stored, and protected.

Another concern is sampling bias. The survey is voluntary and available for only a limited time, meaning that the respondents may not represent the entire population of participants. Those who choose to respond may have particularly strong positive or negative opinions, while others may not participate at all. This imbalance can lead to skewed results, raising concerns about fairness, as decisions based on biased data may unfairly impact courses or instructors.

The survey also lacks sufficient transparency. Participants are not clearly informed about how their responses will be used or whether individual-level data might ever be accessed. This lack of openness can erode trust and discourage honest participation. Ethical data practices require clear communication to ensure participants fully understand how their data contributes to decision-making.

There is also a risk of misinterpretation or misuse of the data. Executive management intends to use the survey results to guide course improvements, but if the data is incomplete or biased, conclusions may be flawed. For example, decision-makers may generalize from a small sample size or mistake correlation for causation. This highlights the importance of accountability, as data professionals must ensure that insights are accurate and responsibly communicated.

Finally, accessibility and inclusivity are important ethical considerations. A limited survey window may exclude participants who are unavailable during that time, and technical barriers may prevent some individuals—particularly those with disabilities—from participating. Ethical systems must ensure that all participants have equal opportunities to contribute their perspectives.

3. Recommendations for Improvement

To address these ethical concerns, several improvements should be implemented:

- Clarify anonymity vs. confidentiality

Remove the login requirement if true anonymity is intended, or clearly state that responses are confidential rather than anonymous. Ensure all claims accurately reflect actual data practices.

- Strengthen informed consent

Provide a clear introduction explaining what data is collected, how it will be used, who will have access, and whether responses can be linked to individuals. Emphasize that participation is voluntary.

- Improve survey design

Use neutral and clearly defined questions, avoid leading language, and include both quantitative and qualitative questions to capture richer insights.

- Reduce bias and improve representation

Extend the survey availability period, encourage broader participation, and monitor response patterns to ensure a more representative sample.

- Implement strong data governance practices

Limit the collection of identifying information, anonymize data where possible, restrict access to authorized personnel, and establish secure storage and retention policies.

- Enhance transparency and communication

Clearly explain the purpose of the survey and how results will be used. Share aggregated findings with participants and demonstrate how feedback leads to meaningful improvements.

- Establish ethical oversight

Regularly review survey practices and ensure alignment with ethical principles such as fairness, accountability, and transparency. Provide training for staff involved in data collection and analysis.

4. Ethical Actions if Recommendations Are Rejected

If executive management refuses to acknowledge these concerns, data professionals still have an ethical obligation to act responsibly. One important step is to document all concerns clearly in reports or communications to ensure accountability and transparency. This creates a record of the risks associated with the current approach.

Another strategy is to frame concerns in terms of organizational impact, such as the risk of losing participant trust, generating low-quality data, or damaging the organization’s reputation. Presenting ethical issues as business risks may make them more compelling to decision-makers.

Data professionals must also maintain integrity in their analysis by clearly communicating limitations, including potential bias and issues with anonymity. They should avoid overstating conclusions and include appropriate disclaimers to ensure findings are not misinterpreted.

If necessary, concerns should be escalated through appropriate channels, such as compliance teams, ethics committees, or legal advisors. Seeking guidance from these bodies can help ensure that ethical standards are upheld.

Ultimately, professionals must adhere to professional responsibility, which means refusing to misrepresent data or support misleading conclusions, even under pressure. Upholding ethical principles is essential to maintaining both personal integrity and organizational credibility.

Conclusion: Ethics Is Not Optional in Data Science

This case highlights a critical truth: ethical challenges often arise in seemingly simple systems. A routine course survey, if poorly designed, can compromise privacy, introduce bias, and erode trust.

Several key lessons emerge:

- Transparency and informed consent are essential

- Anonymity must be genuine and clearly communicated

- Bias and poor design can invalidate data

- Ethical responsibility extends beyond compliance to integrity

As data professionals, our role is not just to analyze information, but to ensure that data is collected and used in a way that respects individuals and produces trustworthy insights. Ethical data science is not only about avoiding harm—it is about building systems that people can rely on with confidence.

In the end, the most important question is not just:

👉 “What can we learn from this data?”

but also:

👉 “Are we collecting and using this data in a way that is fair, honest, and responsible?”

Only when the answer is yes can data truly fulfill its purpose.
